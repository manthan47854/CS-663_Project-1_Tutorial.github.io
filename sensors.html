2. Sensing & Data Acquisition

2.1 Sensor Possibilities

Single RGB camera (baseline)

  • Frame rate: 30–60 fps for general drills; 60–120 fps recommended for sprints, hurdles, jumps, rapid cuts. Higher fps reduces temporal aliasing for contact/flight detection.
  • Resolution: 1080p minimum; 4K preferred when cropping or for multi-person scenes (allows digital zoom while maintaining joint pixel density).
  • Shutter & exposure: Manual exposure; lock auto-exposure/auto-WB to avoid flicker. Use ND filters outdoors to hold fast shutter without overexposing. Disable electronic image stabilization (it warps geometry).
  • Sensor/codec: Prefer global-shutter cameras when available; otherwise keep rolling-shutter readout short (high-end phone “main” lenses usually best). Record constant-frame-rate (CFR) at ≥20 Mbps.

Multi-view RGB (preferred for 3D)

  • 2–4 cameras with overlapping FOV covering the action volume.
  • Maintain baseline (camera spacing) of 1–5 m for close actions (gym) and 5–15 m for field sports.
  • Aim for 30°–90° angle between optical axes (≈60° ideal). Avoid near-colinear setups.

Depth cameras (optional)

  • Time-of-Flight (ToF): Most robust indoors; longer range and fewer pattern-washout issues than structured-light. Typical useful range 0.5–6 m; depth noise grows with distance.
  • Structured-light: Good indoors at short range (<3 m), vulnerable to sunlight.
  • Stereo RGB-D: Works indoors/outdoors with adequate texture; needs higher compute for matching.

IMU fusion (optional)

  • 6-axis or 9-axis IMUs at 100–200 Hz for segment orientation stability during occlusion and fast rotations.
  • Especially valuable for distal segments (feet, wrists) where velocities change abruptly.

Rule of thumb: If the athlete crosses >10 pixels between consecutive frames, increase fps or shutter speed to limit blur and preserve joint localization.

2.2 Camera Placement

View geometry

  • Orthogonal views: sagittal + frontal for running/cutting. For throws/lifts, add oblique (≈45°) to capture transverse plane.
  • Height: mount at hip-to-chest level to reduce perspective distortion; tilt ≤5° unless intentionally top-down.
  • Distance/FOV: subject should occupy 30–60% of frame height; keep full body in frame through the entire motion.
  • Lenses: 24–35 mm equivalent minimizes distortion while preserving FOV. Prefer the phone’s 1× main lens (usually best optics/low RS) over ultrawide.

Exposure guidelines

  • Shutter: ≥1/1000 s for sprints/hurdles/jumps; ≥1/500 s for cutting/field drills; ≥1/250–1/500 s for strength lifts depending on bar speed.
  • ISO/Aperture: raise ISO as needed; add light rather than slowing shutter. Indoors, avoid LED flicker by using shutter multiples of mains frequency (US: ~1/60, 1/120, etc.) when not at very fast shutter.
  • Focus & WB: manual or locked; use a mid-gray reference before capture.

Stability & environment

  • Stability: tripod or rigid clamp. If using a gimbal, lock horizon and disable EIS.
  • Environment & attire: high contrast with background; avoid loose or reflective clothing; ensure shoes and knees are visible (no long skirts/pants that occlude joints).

2.3 Multi-View Calibration & Synchronization

Intrinsics

  • Estimate (fx, fy, cx, cy, k1–k3, p1–p2) via checkerboard or AprilTag grid across the full FOV and at working distances.
  • Re-calibrate after any zoom/focus change.

Extrinsics

  • Solve camera poses (R, t) within the action volume.
  • Static object calibration: checkerboard/AprilTags at multiple depths.
  • Dynamic bundle adjustment: use the athlete’s tracked keypoints across views.

Scale & scene constraints

  • Use known field markings (lane lines, hurdle spacing) for metric scale; optionally refine with ground-plane estimation.

Quality targets

  • Mean reprojection error < 0.8 px in field conditions (≤0.5 px in lab).
  • Essential-matrix inlier ratio >80%.
  • Triangulated point cheirality checks positive.

Temporal sync

  • Best: hardware genlock or LTC timecode.
  • Practical: NTP/PTP-synchronized clocks + clap/LED event; refine via cross-correlation of frame differences/optical-flow energy.
  • Keep drift <1 frame per 5 min; apply time-warp correction if needed.

Rolling-shutter notes

  • If using RS cameras, estimate row readout time and apply RS rectification for fast horizontal motion.

2.4 Depth & IMUs (Fusion Details)

Depth cameras

  • Mount near the RGB camera to simplify extrinsics; avoid specular floors and bright windows in FOV.
  • Enable ambient-light and multipath suppression (if available). Use median/bilateral filters + temporal smoothing; discard depth beyond reliable range.
  • Register depth to RGB using calibrated extrinsics; fill small holes with edge-aware inpainting before 3D lifting.

IMUs

  • Placement: distal segments (feet, wrists) for event timing and peak velocity; shank/thigh/forearm for segment orientation; secure with rigid straps/tape to reduce soft-tissue artifact.
  • Calibration: short T-pose/N-pose for sensor-to-segment alignment; perform static bias calibration pre-session.
  • Fusion: complementary/Madgwick/Mahony as lightweight options; UKF/EKF for tighter coupling with vision (state: segment quaternions, joint angles, gyro/accel biases). Use ZUPT at foot contact to constrain drift. Prefer 6-axis indoors (magnetometer often unreliable).
  • Timing: sample IMUs at 100–200 Hz; timestamp at source; align to video via LED or high-amplitude audio tick.

2.5 Capture Protocol & Practical Tips

  • Record 5–10 s per drill, 3–5 takes each. Include 2 s of still stance at start/end for baseline noise.
  • Slate metadata verbally or with a whiteboard (athlete, drill, load, set/rep, shoes, surface). Use a file naming scheme: {sport}_{athleteID}_{drill}_{date}_{view}.mp4.
  • For outdoor sun, shoot with the sun behind the camera or under overcast; avoid heat shimmer over long baselines.
  • Confirm the athlete remains fully in frame during the movement; rehearse paths once before recording.
  • Disable background apps/notifications on phones to avoid variable frame pacing.
